Framing Sprint#

The framing sprint involved creating a Design Brief for a given opportunity. My teamâ€™s Design Brief framed an opportunity to help U of T students maintain and develop good posture.

An excerpt from the Design Brief created during the Praxis I Framing Sprint showcasing the use of TMFs.

Tools Models And Frameworks (TMFs) Used#

The key TMFs used throughout this sprint were:

  • Scoping: We scoped the opportunity of helping individuals maintain good posture to U of T students who we could gather data from.
  • Requirements model: Design for Safety, Design for Usability and Design for Affordability were the primary high level objectives we prioritized. We did this prioritization using our team and individual values of valuing safety and usability of designs while our experience as university students motivated our prioritization of affordable solutions.
  • The Toulmin Model Of Argumentation: From justifying the selection of metrics and constraints to explaining how existing reference designs were insufficient for tackling the opportunity, this was used in order to write the engineering claims made throughout the brief.
  • The Model Of Co-Reflective Practice: This model helped facilitate team interactions in an effective manner.
Lessons
  • Establishing team values and norms strengthen the work done throughout a project.
  • Checklists and to do lists are effective tools to use for organizing team work.
  • Studying ISO standards and engineering handbooks are a good place to begin when looking for metrics and testing protocols.

Diverging Sprint#

The goal of this sprint was to diverge and develop candidate designs for an opportunity. The brief given to my team was framed as an opportunity to find an alternative to clamshell packaging.

An excerpt from the diverging conducted during the Praxis I Diverging Sprint using key TMFs.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

The key TMFs used throughout this sprint were:

  • Anchoring + Cognitive Bias Avoidance: During diverging, being judgmental towards others inhibits their creative process. An important aspect of the sprint was fostering an environment where teammates could share ideas. I did this through team building activities such as icebreakers, trying to use System 2 thinking, and using a range of diverging tools.
  • BrainWriting 6-3-5 + SCAMPER: We used this divergence tool at the beginning of the sprint since it was an effective way to diverge without anchoring ourselves to other group members' ideas. We augmented our use of Brain Writing by intentionally using SCAMPER transformations by adapting and combining existing solutions or ideas being diverged.
  • Wishing: We used wishing as our second divergence tool. After diverging ideas with one tool, this was a flexible tool that helped us diverge ideas without anchoring us to past ideas. In addition, ideas diverged through wishing did not necessarily have to meet all the requirements and constraints of the given brief. This freed our thinking quite a bit and allowed us to expand the solution space.
  • MORPH Charts: The Morph Chart allowed our group to effectively compare reference designs and develop a prototype for a tool. The final candidate design for the solution was chosen through a combination of elements of the MORPH chart.
  • Challenging Assumptions: When we finished formulating ideas using Brain Writing and Wishing we started to anchor to specific ideas. Challenging assumptions provided us with an excellent opportunity to think outside the box.
Lessons
  • We tried using the Lotus Blossom Technique during this diverging sprint, however, it was not very useful. Part of the reason was because we used it late into diverging when many of the ideas that could go onto a lotus blossom chart were exhausted. In the future, it would be better to try using this tool earlier to test if it is still beneficial to use.

Converging Sprint#

This sprint was centered around converging on one final recommended solution given a set of candidate designs.

An excerpt of the converging tools used and recommended product during the Praxis I Converging Sprint.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

The key TMFs used throughout this sprint were:

  • Multi Voting: Quick and easy way to conduct preliminary convergence when there are too many candidates to consider.
  • Focused BrainWriting: Converging does not mean no diverging is done. This custom made tool leverages BrainWriting 6-3-5 in order to effectively diverge ideas without losing the focus of converging.
  • Force Field Analysis: Medium detail converging tool that is useful to have a more informed decision than Multi Voting while still requiring less time than more in depth converging tools.
  • Proxy Testing: This is necessary when there is insufficient time and/or resources, however, key solutions need to be compared along critical metrics. Instead of comparisons using standardized metrics, this allows for relative comparisons between solutions using a heuristic.
  • PINC Filter: This is a very effective and detailed converging tool that was used to do holistic converging. We used it when there were only 3-4 final solutions that need to be considered.
  • Measurement Matrix And Pugh Chart: This was used to represent the results from testing and research. The Pugh chart was also used to help compare solutions in an easy to visualize manner.
Lessons
  • The principle of iterative design is useful during converging because usually no candidate is worthy of being a final solution. This doesn't mean that the final selection has to be done out of the initially diverged/proposed candidates, or worse, that there are no solutions. Instead, a process of converging, diverging, and repeating those two actions is necessary until a more suitable design materializes.

Request For Proposal (RFP)#

In Praxis II, my team and I created a Request For Proposal (RFP) that framed an engineering opportunity which aimed to recreate a traditional orchestra experience for the Toronto Symphony Orchestra musicians (TSO) during the lockdowns of the COVID-19 pandemic.

An excerpt from the Request For Proposal writing and interview process with stakeholders during Praxis II.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

Many of the TMFs previously used were also applied in writing this RFP. However, the key TMFs used throughout the writing of the RFP were:

  • PIAA Model And Stakeholder Engagement: We had the opportunity to interact with a community. Through the paradigm of participatory design as well as using the PIAA Model we were able to observe the workings of a community, interpret those observations and assess opportunities that existed within them.
  • Requirements model: We used this to detail the objectives valued by the stakeholders and my team as well as identify metrics, criteria and constraints needed to do fulfill these objectives.
Lessons
  • Field notes helped during and after stakeholder engagements. My team and I all took field notes which we compared after stakeholder meetings. This gave us a multiplicity of views, observations and interpretations about our conversation. This made it more likely that we actually understood what was being communicated.

Showcase#

The final phase of Praxis II involved going through the entire FDCR process in order to develop a solution to a particular RFP. My team and I were given an RFP that sought to develop a solution that assisted visually impaired people identify clothing items.

The product prototyped, validated by the stakeholders and presented at the Praxis II showcase.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

Many of the TMFs used in the Praxis I sprints were also applied in preparing for Showcase. However, the key TMFs used throughout preparing for and delivering showcase were:

  • Prototyping: I prototyped concepts in a low fidelity manner in order to communicate ideas to my team. Additionally, I made a medium fidelity prototype for testing and demonstration purposes at Showcase.
  • Stakeholder Validation: After verifying our solutions using prototyping and testing protocols, we validated the usability of our design ideas using a Wizard Of Oz testing protocol. We also validated the concept through pointed questions towards our stakeholder and got a holistic assessment of our work from their comments that stemmed from personal experience.
Lessons
  • Avoid the curse of knowledge. In a presentation, presuming the audience knows too much or overloading them with details both hinder communication. Balancing the two is challenging but a good heuristic for whether it is being done is through doing test runs with other people and letting them give you honest feedback about how your presentation went.

Building a Bridge in CIV102#

One of the final projects in CIV102 was to design a bridge using mat-board. Although we didn't get to physically build and test our bridge due to COVID-19 restrictions, this project was an opportunity to work on a technical design in a team and do a lot of rigorous calculations to ensure the design could bear load and avoid failures such as buckling and bending.

One of the cross sections of a bridge design being subject to a bending moment analysis.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

The key TMFs used to build the bridge included:

  • Engineering Calculations, Handbooks and Standards: The methods of modeling bending, shear buckling and plate buckling amongst other bridge failures were used to test the integrity of our design.
  • Visual Representations: This was key to ensuring that everyone on the team was envisioning the same thing. Quick sketches were integral to the success of this project.
Lessons
  • Although we did not get to test this part of the project, from the past experience of upper years and conversations with TAs such as Allan Kuan, historically, whenever people have put their bridges to the test they have found that they often overestimated the final load the bridge could bear when doing their calculations. The physical test and the eventual breaking of the bridge before their eyes is a visual reminder of the importance of rigorous calculations, factors of safety, conservative estimates to ensure the integrity of a design and the need for real world testing.

Developing The Pedestrian Detection System Of A Self Driving Car In aUToronto#

I conducted pedestrian detection research at aUToronto. The focus of this project was improving the existing pedestrian detection system which was based off of a neural network architecture known as Squeezdet and replace it with a YoloV3 network architecture which has been found to perform better with newly trained weights and fine tuned hyper-parameters.

The trained YoloV3 model detections on pedestrians being tested on our self driving car at UTIAS, U of T.

Tools Models And Frameworks Used#

The key TMFs used to build the pedestrian detection system included:

  • Creating a feedback loop between testing solutions and validating them with stakeholders, which in this case would be the team leads that test the trained weights on Zeus, our self driving car, was essential.
  • Triangulating research is also important to do as there is a lot of experience that can be gained through small scale experiments with our system, however corroborating these findings with the literature that also studies these phenomena is useful for improving the credibility of the solutions being developed.
Lessons
  • It was crucial to plan ahead in this project. This is because training a model such as YoloV3 on large amounts of self driving car data takes a minimum of 24 hours on a GPU. This is a significant time investment which would hinder rapid testing if the hyper-parameters we were tuning and testing were not planned in advance.
